# Tao Te Ching | 도덕경 (道德經)

> The Classic of the Way and Its Virtue

---

## Overview | 개요

The Tao Te Ching (道德經, Dàodéjīng), literally "Classic of the Way and Its Virtue," is an ancient Chinese classic text that became the foundational work of Taoism. Traditionally credited to the sage Laozi, it is one of the most-often translated texts in the world, with the title rendered variously as "The Way and Its Power," "The Way of Virtue," and "Classics of Integrity and the Way."

In just 81 brief chapters and approximately 5,000 Chinese characters, it presents a profound philosophy of living in harmony with the natural order—the Tao.

## Laozi (老子) | 노자

> "Laozi was a legendary Chinese philosopher considered to be the author of the Tao Te Ching. The name, literally meaning 'Old Master,' was likely intended to portray an archaic anonymity."

### Traditional Account:
> "Traditional accounts describe him as Li Er, born in the 6th-century BCE state of Chu. Serving as the royal archivist for the Zhou court, he met and impressed Confucius on one occasion, composing the Tao Te Ching in a single session before retiring into the western wilderness."

### Historical Status:
Whether Laozi was a historical figure or a legendary composite, the text bearing his name has profoundly shaped Chinese civilization.

## Wu Wei (無為) | 무위

> "Wu wei, literally 'non-action' or 'not acting,' is a central concept of the Tao Te Ching. It can mean 'not doing anything,' 'not forcing,' 'acting spontaneously,' and 'flowing with the moment.'"

### What It Really Means:
> "Wu wei does not really mean that you do nothing. Rather it means natural action—action that does not involve struggle or excessive effort. Wu wei is the cultivation of a mental state in which our actions are quite effortlessly in alignment with the flow of life."

### Two Layers:
> "Wu wei contains two layers: the first advocates following the 'law of nature' and not destroying harmony. The second advocates that human activities should be natural and flow without being forced."

### Water as Model:
The Tao Te Ching famously uses water as the supreme example of wu wei:

> "The supreme good is like water, which nourishes all things without trying to. It is content with the low places that people disdain."

> "무위(無為)는 문자 그대로 '행위하지 않음' 또는 '작위하지 않음'이며, 도덕경의 중심 개념입니다."
### 실제 의미:
> "무위는 정말로 아무것도 하지 않는다는 의미가 아닙니다. 오히려 자연스러운 행위—투쟁이나 과도한 노력이 없는 행위—를 의미합니다."

## Key Teachings | 핵심 가르침

### 1. Return to Nature
> "Many people act 'unnaturally,' upsetting the natural balance of the Tao, and the text intends to lead students to a 'return' to their natural state, in harmony with Tao."

### 2. Embracing Paradox
- The soft overcomes the hard
- Emptiness creates usefulness
- By yielding, one conquers
- In weakness is strength

### 3. Simplicity
> "Laozi encouraged a return to 'nature,' rather than action. The answer is not the rejection of technology, but seeking the calm state of wu wei, free from desires."

### 4. The Sage
The ideal ruler or person acts without interfering:

> "Governing a great nation is like cooking a small fish—too much handling will spoil it."

### 1. 자연으로의 귀환
> "많은 사람들이 '부자연스럽게' 행동하여 도의 자연적 균형을 깨뜨립니다. 텍스트는 학생들을 도와 조화로운 자연 상태로 '귀환'하도록 이끕니다."

## Famous Passages | 유명한 구절

### Opening:
> "The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao." (道可道非常道)

### On Water:
> "The highest good is like water. Water benefits all things and does not compete."

### On Wu Wei:
> "The sage acts without action, teaches without speaking." (聖人處無為之事，行不言之教)

### On Paradox:
> "In the pursuit of learning, every day something is acquired. In the pursuit of Tao, every day something is dropped."

### 시작:
> "도라 할 수 있는 도는 상도가 아니다." (道可道非常道)
### 물에 대해:
> "상선은 물과 같다. 물은 만물을 이롭게 하고 다투지 않는다."
### 무위에 대해:
> "성인은 무위로써 일을 처리하고, 말 없는 가르침을 행한다."
