# Moksha | 목샤 (解脫)

> Liberation of the Soul: The Ultimate Goal

---

## Overview | 개요

Moksha (Sanskrit: मोक्ष, mokṣa), also called vimoksha, vimukti, and mukti, is a term in Hinduism, Jainism, Buddhism, and Sikhism for various forms of emancipation, liberation, or release. In its soteriological and eschatological senses, it refers to freedom from saṃsāra, the cycle of death and rebirth.

In Hindu traditions, moksha is a central concept and the utmost aim of human life. The other three aims are dharma (virtuous life), artha (material prosperity), and kama (pleasure). Together, these four concepts are called the Puruṣārtha—the four goals of human existence.

---
## The Soul and Liberation | 영혼과 해방

### The Atman:
Moksha in Hindu philosophy generally affirms the existence of an eternal, imperishable soul (atman) that realizes its fundamental unity with Brahman (supreme reality).

### The Liberation Experience:
> "After attaining moksha, the liberated soul experiences infinite consciousness (sat), complete knowledge (chit), and eternal bliss (ananda), totally freed from all limitations and suffering."

This is sometimes expressed as Sat-Chit-Ananda—Being, Consciousness, Bliss.

### 아트만:
### 해탈 경험:

## Paths to Moksha | 목샤의 길

> "Moksha is achieved through four classical paths: Karma Yoga (selfless action), Bhakti Yoga (devotion), Jnana Yoga (knowledge), and Raja Yoga (meditation), as revealed in the Bhagavad Gita and Upanishads."

| Path | Focus | Method |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Karma Yoga** | Action | Selfless service without attachment to results |
| **Bhakti Yoga** | Devotion | Love and surrender to the divine |
| **Jnana Yoga** | Knowledge | Discrimination between real and unreal |
| **Raja Yoga** | Meditation | Control of mind through yogic practices |

### Requirements:
> "To achieve moksha, one must free themselves from material anxieties and desires. One must also develop a deep knowledge of the soul, and understand the universe. This is done through meditation, as well as fulfilling one's dharma."

## Vedanta Interpretations | 베단타 해석

Different philosophical schools within Vedanta interpret moksha distinctly:

| School | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Advaita Vedanta** | Complete non-duality—individual soul and Brahman are absolutely one |
| **Vishishtadvaita** | Qualified non-duality—soul retains subtle distinction |
| **Dvaita** | Eternal distinction—liberated soul serves God eternally |

### Shankara's View:
> "Shankara's Advaita Vedanta asserts the non-dualistic nature of reality, positing that the individual self (Atman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman) are ultimately identical."

| 학파 | 해석 |
|-----|-----|
| **아드바이타 베단타** | 완전한 비이원성—개별 영혼과 브라만은 절대적으로 하나 |
| **비시슈타드바이타** | 제한적 비이원성—영혼이 미묘한 구별을 유지 |
| **드바이타** | 영원한 구별—해방된 영혼이 영원히 신을 섬김 |

## Liberation: In Life or After | 해탈: 생전 또는 사후

### Jivanmukti (Liberation While Living):
> "In Advaita Vedanta philosophy, a jivanmukta has attained a deep realisation of his sense of self and that of the universe. Therefore, a jivanmukta is also called Atma Jnani (one who possesses the knowledge of his self) and Brahma Jnani (one who has attained the sense of the universe)."

### Videhamukti (Liberation After Death):
> "At the end of their lives, jivanmuktas attain paramukti (final liberation)."

### Schools' Differences:
> "Samkhya and Yoga schools consider moksha as possible in this life. The Advaita sub-school concludes moksha is possible in this life, while Dvaita, Visistadvaita sub-schools believe that moksha is a continuous event that extends from this life to post-mortem."

### 지반묵티 (생전 해탈):
> "아드바이타 베단타 철학에서 지반묵타는 자아와 우주에 대한 깊은 깨달음을 얻은 사람입니다."
### 비데하묵티 (사후 해탈):
> "지반묵타들은 생의 마지막에 파라묵티(최종 해탈)를 얻습니다."
